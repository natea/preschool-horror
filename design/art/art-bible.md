*Created: 2026-04-08*

## 1. Visual Identity Statement

### Master Visual Rule

> **"This space was built for children who trusted it — make sure it still looks that way, and make sure it isn't."**

This resolves any visual ambiguity in one question: Does this asset preserve the preschool's surface promise of safety while containing something that quietly betrays it? If the wrongness is too obvious, pull back. If the safety is too convincing, add corruption. The horror lives in the gap, not at either extreme.

### Supporting Visual Principles

**Principle 1: The Camera Tells the Truth the Room Hides**
*Serves Pillar 2 — "Prove It"*

Anomalies are visible to the naked eye. The camera's job is confirmation, not revelation. Anomalies must read as subtly wrong in the environment — off enough to notice, ambiguous enough to second-guess. The photograph, once captured, locks truth into evidence.

*Design test*: If a player could walk past this anomaly without noticing it on Night 1, it's calibrated correctly. If it's obviously wrong from across the room, dial the corruption down.

**Principle 2: Familiar Shapes, Foreign Behavior**
*Serves Pillar 1 — "Something's Wrong Here"*

Every asset in the preschool — furniture, toys, decorations, lighting — must read as plausible at a glance. Low-poly modeling works in our favor: simplified geometry mimics the imprecision of memory. A chair with slightly wrong proportions isn't a bad model — it's a memory of a chair. Horror enters only through behavior and placement, never through form alone.

*Design test*: If the asset would be unremarkable in a preschool photo on Pinterest, the form is correct. Corruption belongs in placement, orientation, state, and animation — not silhouette or shape.

**Principle 3: Color Debt**
*Serves Pillar 4 — "One More Night"*

The preschool's palette begins fully saturated — primary Crayola colors, clean whites, warm yellows. Each night the palette incurs debt: pastels shift toward desaturated mids, warm tones cool. By Night 5-6, sickly green-gray and deep violet have colonized the space. This is selective decay — healthy colors remain in spots, surrounded by encroaching wrongness, making it feel like a spreading infection rather than a mood filter.

Monsters break the palette completely: void black, unnatural white, arterial red. Their palette wrongness signals "this does not belong to the same world" before the player consciously processes the threat.

*Design test*: Desaturate any night's screenshot. If Night 1 and Night 6 look identical in grayscale value distribution, the color debt escalation isn't working. Night 6 should be visibly darker and lower-contrast in value, not just shifted in hue.

### Pillar Alignment

| Principle | Pillar Served | What It Prevents |
|---|---|---|
| Familiar Shapes, Foreign Behavior | "Something's Wrong Here" | Assets that read as horror props rather than preschool objects |
| The Camera Tells the Truth the Room Hides | "Prove It" | Anomalies that are invisible or screaming-obvious |
| Color Debt | "One More Night" | Nights that feel tonally identical; monsters that blend in |

> Note: Pillar 3 ("Trust No One") is served through narrative and UI design (Section 7), not environment art.

## 2. Mood & Atmosphere

### Exploration — Nights 1-2 (The Quiet Before)

**Primary emotion:** Suspicious comfort. The player feels almost safe, which is the problem.

**Lighting:** Warm incandescent, 3200K. Low contrast — soft shadows that don't commit to edges. Fluorescent overheads flickering at 5-10% intensity variation (faulty-ballast flicker, barely perceptible). Supplementary warm fill from streetlight orange through high windows. Shadows bottom out at deep warm gray, never black.

**Descriptors:** Hushed. Familiar. Overlit-for-emptiness. Almost-fine. Still.

**Energy:** 1/5. A sleeping building, not a threatening one.

**Key visual element:** The room does not look like a horror game. It looks like a preschool that forgot to turn the lights off. Overheads illuminate evenly — nothing hides. The wrongness is fully visible, bathed in the same warm light as everything else.

### Exploration — Nights 3-4 (The First Wrongness)

**Primary emotion:** Alert dread. The player knows they are no longer alone.

**Lighting:** Cooled to 4500K neutral-white with isolated warm pockets. Contrast increased — shadows commit to edges. At least one overhead dead per room, creating ceiling-level darkness the floor practicals cannot reach. Light comes from below-average height, elongating shadows upward.

**Descriptors:** Watched. Uneven. Cooling. Held-breath. Cornered.

**Energy:** 2.5/5. Controlled movement, deliberate pauses. The player scans doorways.

**Key visual element:** Small monsters (living dolls, shadow figures at child height) occupy the same warm-lit zones as furniture. Their void-black and unnatural-white palette reads as wrong even before conscious identification — Color Debt paying out in creature form.

### Exploration — Nights 5-7 (Full Debt Collected)

**Primary emotion:** Survival dread. Active threat. "Something is happening."

**Lighting:** 5500K-6500K cold blue-white, clinical. High contrast. Surviving practicals are islands of color in dominant cool. Luminance lower than prior nights — areas fall below comfortable visibility. Ceiling permanently dark. Night 7: near-total cool dominance, the last warm practical in each room is a deliberate visual orphan that makes the cold feel colder.

**Descriptors:** Hostile. Fragmented. Cornered. Cold. Breaking-down.

**Energy:** 4/5. Sustained tension with burst moments.

**Key visual element:** Finger-paintings on walls — full-saturation primary colors on Night 1, now desaturated to near-gray with only isolated patches of original color. They function as calibration objects across all nights. A Night 6 screenshot should be visibly different in value distribution from Night 1.

### Camera Viewfinder Mode

**Primary emotion:** Focused vulnerability. The player has narrowed their world to a rectangle.

**Lighting:** Subtle post-process: mild vignette tightens peripheral field, colors inside the frame desaturate ~10-15%. Flash produces single-frame full-room overexposure at 6500K pure white, bleaching all color for a half-second then snapping back. In the flash frame, low-visibility anomalies become briefly, starkly visible — then gone.

**Descriptors:** Tunnel-vision. Precise. Exposed. Caught-in-amber. Revealing.

**Energy:** 3/5 on Nights 1-2, scaling to 4.5/5 on Nights 5-7.

**Key visual element:** The viewfinder has physical properties: slight lens distortion at edges, real-time depth-of-field pull when zooming, and a faint reflection of the player's eye in the glass when looking at bright surfaces. Subtle enough to miss on first pass — when noticed in a screenshot, it recontextualizes every photo taken. The camera has been looking back.

### Boss Debrief (Morning After)

**Primary emotion:** False safety. Warm authority. The most comfortable the player feels — and the most deceived they are.

**Lighting:** Warmest state in the game. 2700K. Desk lamp as key light, soft upward fill onto the boss. The only space lit for a human occupant — the preschool is lit for absence, this room for presence. Deep warm shadows, not cold. Each night, the light on the boss's face dims by one stop from the previous night — the only room where Color Debt applies to a character.

**Descriptors:** Warm. Paternal. Legible. Falsely-resolved. Amber.

**Energy:** 1/5. Deliberate deceleration. The game lets the player breathe — and lowers their guard.

**Key visual element:** The boss's face is always slightly too close to the light source — a half-stop too bright, reading as minor uncanny in the low-poly style. Not enough to trigger immediate alarm. On second playthrough, it reads clearly as "Familiar Shapes, Foreign Behavior" applied to a character. By Night 6, the dimming is perceptible.

### Title / Main Menu

**Primary emotion:** Curious unease. Tone-set before the player knows the rules.

**Lighting:** Still image. A single preschool hallway, shot from child height. Nights 1-2 warm baseline. One fluorescent at the far end is out, creating a single dark zone. What is standing in that dark zone is not legible.

**Descriptors:** Quiet. Childlike-in-scale. Recognizable. Off-by-one. Anticipatory.

**Energy:** 0.5/5. Pre-tension. The last moment of calm.

**Key visual element:** The game title renders as a child's hand-lettered crayon sign — the kind hung above a classroom door — but the letters have been subtly rearranged. The original configuration spells something different than "Show & Tell." A discovery element that rewards Explorers who return after the twist.

### Night-to-Night Color Temperature Summary

| State | Color Temp | Contrast | Dominant Hue Shift | Energy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nights 1-2 | 3200K warm | Low-soft | Warm yellow-orange | 1/5 |
| Nights 3-4 | 4500K neutral | Medium | Cooling, neutralizing | 2.5/5 |
| Nights 5-7 | 5500-6500K cold | High | Blue-gray, violet | 4/5 |
| Viewfinder | Inherits room + flash overexpose | Vignette-tightened | Desaturated -10-15% | Scales |
| Boss Debrief | 2700K warmest | Cozy-high | Deep amber (dims nightly) | 1/5 |
| Title Screen | 3200K baseline | Medium | One cold void zone | 0.5/5 |

## 3. Shape Language

### 3.1 The Geometric Vocabulary of the Preschool

**Dominant forms: rounded rectangles, squat cylinders, blunt arches.**

Preschool furniture exists in the world of child-safe geometry: table corners are radiused, doorways are proportioned for small bodies, cubbies are wide and low. Every structural element uses one of three base forms:

- **Squat rectangles** (tables, cubbies, storage bins) — low center of gravity, wide footprint, reassuring solidity
- **Cylinders and ovals** (columns, stools, rounded wall corners) — no sharp entry, softened transitions
- **Blunt arches** (doorways, window cutouts, nap room alcoves) — a shape children associate with safety

Low-poly execution reinforces this: faceted geometry that approximates roundness reads as the memory of a round shape, not its reality. This serves "Familiar Shapes, Foreign Behavior" — the form is right enough to trust, the precision is wrong enough to unsettle.

**Why this serves the horror:** Rounded geometry is the visual language of "this will not hurt you." Filling an entire space with child-safe geometry and then placing threats inside it creates maximum dissonance. The shapes promised safety. They lied.

*Pillar alignment: Pillar 1 — "Something's Wrong Here."*

### 3.2 Character and Monster Silhouette Philosophy

**Rule: Every monster silhouette must read at thumbnail size and must never overlap with a human silhouette.**

| Monster Tier | Silhouette Word | Geometry Rule | Design Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| Living Dolls (small) | Stumpy | Child-height, oversized head, rigid limbs at wrong angles | Human proportion but wrong — joints don't move like a child's |
| Shadow Figures (medium) | Stretched | Tall, narrow, attenuated limbs — negative space dominant | Pure silhouette creature; no surface detail needed |
| Larger Creatures (large, Night 5+) | Splayed | Wide horizontal mass, low center, asymmetric | Breaks vertical bias of human silhouettes; reads as "not a person" instantly |

**The Human Silhouette is sacred:** The player never encounters a human NPC in the preschool at night. If a silhouette reads as a normal person standing normally, it is not a monster — it is an anomaly. A living doll standing like a person is the anomaly. The doll moving like a doll reveals the monster.

**Mixamo sourcing note:** Mixamo characters arrive with human-normalized proportions. Monster archetypes require scale manipulation and bone deformation: stumpy dolls need ~1.5x head scale and ~0.7x limb length; shadow figures need ~1.4x height with ~1.2x arm reach. These ratios are starting points — validate against the actual Mixamo rig before locking as hard specs.

*Pillar alignment: Pillar 2 — "Prove It." Silhouette clarity makes "is this worth photographing?" a real question.*

### 3.3 Anomaly Shape Rules — How Wrongness Uses Geometry

**Core principle: Anomalies disrupt the environment's shape grammar without replacing it.**

**Mode A — Axis Violation (Nights 1-3)**
Objects on the wrong axis. A stack of blocks arranged vertically when they should be horizontal. A chair facing a wall. A drawing pinned upside-down. Geometry unchanged — only orientation is corrupted. The eye reads structural logic; axis violation breaks physics-logic without breaking visual plausibility.

**Mode B — Proximity Collapse (Nights 2-5)**
Objects that have moved into each other's personal space. Toys arranged too close together, facing inward in a circle. Chairs gathered around an empty center. The Gestalt principle of proximity makes objects too close together read as a group with intent. The player feels watched before seeing anything watching.

**Mode C — Shape Intrusion (Nights 3+)**
Geometry that does not belong to the preschool vocabulary. A limb behind a cubby too long to be a child's. A shadow with an edge too sharp for the soft geometry around it. A mark on a drawing using angles no crayon produces. Always partial — the impossible shape half-concealed by expected shapes. Sharp angles in a rounded world.

*Pillar alignment: Principle 1 — "The Camera Tells the Truth the Room Hides." Mode A and B anomalies are walkable-past on first exposure. Mode C anomalies are partial reveals completed by the camera flash.*

### 3.4 UI Shape Grammar — The Viewfinder Language

**Rule: The HUD uses preschool shape grammar. The viewfinder breaks it.**

**Preschool HUD (inventory, night counter, photo count):**
Rounded rectangles with thick borders matching furniture proportions. Construction-paper aesthetic — blocky, imprecise, generous margins. Hand-lettered font: readable, chunky, slightly uneven. Colors follow Color Debt — Night 1 HUD is primary-saturated; by Night 6 it's desaturated and cooler.

*This signals: you are in the preschool's world. The UI was made here.*

**Viewfinder (camera mode only):**
Hard right-angle corners — no radius, no softness. Single-pixel crosshair lines. Fixed-width monospace readouts. Segmented arc aperture indicator.

*The viewfinder does not belong to the preschool. It belongs to you — the investigator, the adult. Hard geometry is your anchor in a space that has lost its rules.*

*Pillar alignment: Pillar 2 — "Prove It." The preschool's soft shapes are the lie; the viewfinder's hard angles cut through it.*

### 3.5 Hero Shapes vs. Supporting Shapes

**The player's eye should travel this path in every room:**
1. Door (entry — orienting anchor)
2. Light sources (instinctive survival scanning)
3. Movement or shape-disruption (anomaly — if present)
4. Exit (safety — noted and filed)

- **Camera viewfinder** is always hero when raised: hard geometry, high contrast, centered.
- **Anomalies are aspiring heroes**: visually distinct enough to reward scanning, but not so dominant the player cannot miss them on first pass. An anomaly earns hero status when the player stops and looks — not when they enter the room.
- **Furniture and props** recede through material flatness (low-poly, minimal texture variation) and value proximity to background.
- **The boss's desk** inverts the hierarchy: desk is hero shape, boss is the anomaly within it — slightly too prominent, lit too bright, placed too close.

*Pillar alignment: Pillar 1 — "Something's Wrong Here." The preschool never shouts. It waits to be noticed.*

## 4. Color System

### 4.1 Primary Palette

| Color | Hex | Role | Decay Behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Crayola Yellow** | `#F5C842` | Trust, normalcy, the promise of morning. Overhead light spill, construction paper suns, boss's desk lamp. | Cools toward `#C4BF8A` (muddy khaki). Last color standing under practicals. |
| **Crayola Red** | `#E8392A` | Childhood energy, now actively dangerous. Finger-paintings, blocks, crayon labels. | **Does not decay.** Stays saturated while everything fades — progressively isolated as a threat signal. |
| **Crayola Blue** | `#3B72C8` | Calm authority, observation. Ruled paper, educational posters, boss credentials. | Decays earliest/fastest → `#4B5E6B` (cold blue-gray). By Night 5, looks like a bruise. |
| **Crayola Green** | `#48B04A` | Healthy, growing things. Grass, leaves in drawings, go-signal. | → `#7A8C3A` (sickly yellow-green) → `#5A6450` (mold gray-green). Primary infection color. |
| **Chalk White** | `#F2EDE4` | Cleanliness, blank-slate safety. The walls. Warm paper tint on Night 1. | → `#D8D8DC` (cold institutional white). Warmth gone by Night 5. |
| **Deep Shadow** | `#2A1F1A` | Night 1-2 shadows. Warm dark, not threatening. | → `#1A1A22` (cool violet-tinged black) by Night 5-7. |
| **Infection Violet** | `#6B4E8A` | The corruption hue. Does not exist on Night 1. | Appears Night 3 in shadows, intensifies through Night 7. Purely wrong — no childhood association. |

### 4.2 Night-by-Night Decay Rules

Decay is not a global filter — it is selective, material-level, mimicking infection spreading from dark areas outward.

**Night 1 (Baseline):** All materials at full saturation. Shadows warm `#2A1F1A`. No violet. Finger-paintings at 100% serve as calibration objects.

**Night 2 (First Whisper):** Shadow floors +5% cool shift (imperceptible individually). Greens on far walls shift 8% toward yellow-green. Blues lose 5% saturation. Designed to be indistinguishable from Night 1 to most players.

**Night 3 (The Cooling):** Shadows clearly cooler `#251E28` with violet undertone. Greens visibly yellowed. Blues dropped 15% saturation. Infection Violet in deep corners at ~20% opacity. First monsters appear — their palette makes the environment feel different by contrast.

**Night 4 (Spreading):** Calibration finger-paintings show first decay — red unchanged, yellow dulled to `#D4B038`, blue desaturated to `#7090A8`, green shifted to `#6A8840`. Violet at 40% in shadows. Overhead light cools from 3200K to 4500K.

**Night 5 (Majority Infected):** Green fully transitioned — nothing reads as Crayola Green anymore. Blue is gray-blue bruise. Walls now `#D8D8DC`. Only Red and Yellow (under practicals) remain near-original. Violet dominant in shadows `#3D2F4A`. The visual tipping point.

**Night 6 (Near-Complete):** Yellow survives only directly under practicals. Red stands alone in full saturation — isolated, wrong. Finger-paintings: only red patches remain saturated, surrounded by desaturated ghosts.

**Night 7 (Collapse):** Maximum Color Debt. Only saturated surfaces: surviving practicals, red elements, monster palette. Everything else in cool gray-violet range. The preschool is unrecognizable from Night 1 despite identical geometry.

**Decay Order (fastest to slowest):**
1. Shadow temperatures (Night 2 start)
2. Green surfaces (Night 2 start, complete Night 5)
3. Blue surfaces (Night 2 start, complete Night 6)
4. Yellow indirect/ambient (Night 4 start, Night 6 local-only)
5. White/neutral (Night 3 start, Night 5 complete)
6. Red — **does not decay**
7. Yellow under practicals (last to go, Night 7 under threat)

### 4.3 Monster Palette

Monsters occupy a separate color register outside the preschool's dynamic range.

| Color | Hex | Usage | Why It's Wrong |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Void Black** | `#0A0A0F` | Body masses, open mouths, space behind eyes | Below the shadow floor of any night — shouldn't exist indoors under any lighting |
| **Unnatural White** | `#F0F0FF` | Eyes, exposed surfaces, teeth/joints on dolls | Cooler and brighter than any surface white — reads as "illuminated from inside" |
| **Arterial Red** | `#C0181E` | Wounds/detail marks, eye color, activation states | Darker and cooler than Crayola Red — dried blood rather than fresh paint |

**The contrast mechanism:** On Nights 1-2, monster palette vs. Crayola palette is violent and obvious. On Nights 5-7, the preschool has decayed toward the monster palette's range — players cannot always tell at a glance whether a dark shape is environmental or a monster. Color Debt paying out as a gameplay mechanic.

### 4.6 Colorblind Safety

| Color Pair | Risk | Backup 1 | Backup 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Red vs Green | Deuteranopia / Protanopia (~8% of males) | Shape: angular=danger, rounded=safe | Sound: low pulse vs high ping |
| Red vs Black | Protanopia | White outline on danger-state monsters | Angular halo shape |
| Green vs Yellow | Tritanopia | Letter grade label (A/B/C/D/F) | Numerical score |
| Violet (atmosphere) | Achromatopsia (rare) | Audio distortion is primary cue | Atmospheric, not blocking |

All semantic color pairs must include at least one non-color backup. Color reinforces — it never replaces.

## 5. Character Design Direction

### 5.1 Player Character — First-Person Visual Treatment

The player character has no body — the investigator is a consciousness holding a camera. Absence of a visible body removes the safety of self-reference.

**Hands:** Single hand and forearm, shown only when raising the camera. 80-120 triangles. No fingernail detail. Flat-shaded, desaturated warm mid-value (`#C4997A`), no texture map. A simple neutral cuff — no brand, no color, no personality.

**Camera model:** 200-300 triangles. Chunky 1990s point-and-shoot silhouette. Matte black body with warm cream accents (`#D4C8A0`). When lowered: visible lower-right at 30% viewport. When raised: fills center, viewfinder mode activates.

**Flashlight:** None. The camera flash is the player's only burst-light source. A persistent flashlight would undermine the vulnerability of navigating by ambient light.

### 5.2 Monster Design Direction

**Rule:** All archetypes use only the monster palette (Void Black `#0A0A0F`, Unnatural White `#F0F0FF`, Arterial Red `#C0181E`). No preschool colors on any monster surface.

**Living Dolls — "Stumpy"**
- Corrupted childhood comfort. Toys that something wore.
- Head 1.4-1.6x oversized, limbs ~0.75x standard length, wide stance
- Seam lines at joints rendered as dark edge loops. Flat matte, no specularity
- Eyes: Two Unnatural White dots. No iris, no blink.
- Arterial Red: one detail per doll only (a painted mouth, a worn patch, a button)
- Animation: Head snaps in 15-degree increments. Rigid joints, 60% human bend range. Idle: micro-tremor (2-3mm positional jitter at 8hz) — something trying to hold still

**Shadow Figures — "Stretched"**
- Pure threat geometry. Silhouettes with intent.
- 1.4-1.5x standard height, arms reach below knee, shoulder width 0.6x
- Void Black body, zero interior detail. Unnatural White in eye-zone only (faint internal glow, no geometry source)
- No texture map. Single solid material. Camera flash: same silhouette, overexposed rim only
- Animation: Fluid, no weight, no footfall. Slides rather than walks. Turns by dissolving and re-forming (delegate to technical-artist: shader-based transition). Idle: constant slow sway, underwater quality

**Larger Creatures — "Splayed"**
- Wrong scale, wrong axis. Should not fit in a preschool hallway.
- Wide horizontal mass, low center of gravity, asymmetric (L/R independent)
- Void Black base, Unnatural White on exposed joints/protrusions, Arterial Red in creases/concavities
- Three-material approach: `mat_monster_void`, `mat_monster_white`, `mat_monster_red`
- Animation: Irregular cadence (two fast steps, one pause, one drag). Asymmetric idle. Does not mirror left/right.

### 5.3 Boss Character — Trustworthy Then Sinister

The boss is the only human character. Debrief scenes only. Must read as trustworthy authority first playthrough, anomaly second.

**First read (trustworthy):**
- Standard adult proportions, no monster palette, no deformation
- 2700K desk lamp warmth (Section 2), muted professional wardrobe (deep navy, warm gray)
- Calm low-poly face. Readable. Not threatening.

**Embedded sinister signals (second playthrough):**
- Face 0.5 stops over-lit relative to scene — accumulating uncanniness
- Hands never visible in debrief (camera always frames at desk before hands enter)
- Dialogue UI uses `#8B2020` (dark red) — monster palette bleeding through
- Shadow cast from desk lamp does not match his geometry (hand-authored, not auto-computed)
- Light on face dims one stop per night (Section 2 Color Debt applied to character)

### 5.4 LOD Philosophy

| Asset Category | Hero (0-3m) | Mid (3-6m) | Far (6m+) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Living Dolls | 400-600 tris | 200-300 tris | 100 tris (silhouette only) |
| Shadow Figures | 200-300 tris | 150 tris | 80 tris |
| Larger Creatures | 600-900 tris | 400 tris | 200 tris |
| Boss | 300-500 tris | N/A | N/A |
| Player hand/camera | 300-420 tris | N/A | N/A |

LOD transitions preserve silhouette — interior edge loops removed, outline maintained. Target total room polygon budget with all monsters: under 15,000 triangles. Monster tris should not exceed 30% of room budget.

### 5.5 Mixamo Integration Guidelines

**Required changes (all archetypes):**
1. Remove all Mixamo texture maps → flat single-color monster palette materials
2. Retopology to LOD 0 targets (stock Mixamo: 10K-30K tris, far over budget)
3. Strip facial bones and blend shapes — no facial animation
4. Rename bones: `[archetype]_[bone_name]` (e.g., `doll_spine`, `shadow_arm_upper_L`)

**Archetype deformation targets:**

| Archetype | Head Scale | Limb Length | Key Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Living Dolls | +40-60% | -25% | Widen stance via hip bone rotation. Lock bend range to 60% human max. |
| Shadow Figures | Narrow -30% | +40-50% arm reach | Elongate via spine stretch, not scale. |
| Larger Creatures | Preserve | Asymmetric L/R | Apply asymmetry at mesh level before rig import. |

**Animation post-processing:**
- Dolls: "walking"/"idle" base → add joint constraints + procedural jitter
- Shadows: "floating" or walking with foot IK disabled → smooth all curves, add procedural sway
- Large: "creeping walk" base → irregular cadence via animation blend tree with random-interval weights

**Asset naming:** `/assets/art/characters/[archetype]/char_[archetype]_[state]_[variant].[ext]`

## 6. Environment Design Language

### 6.1 Architectural Style

**Era:** American suburban preschool, built mid-1980s to early 1990s. Single-story. Designed by a school district committee — functional, earnest, slightly too cheerful.

**Condition:** Maintained but not renovated. Linoleum clean but worn in traffic paths. Paint touched up one too many times. Radiator covers bolted with original hardware. Used by children for decades — the building has absorbed that fact.

**Scale:** Built for small bodies. Structural ceilings at adult height, but all fixtures 15-25% lower than adult-normal. Door handles at hip height. Windows starting at 60cm. The player — an adult — feels subtly oversized and out of place.

### 6.2 Texture Philosophy

**Approach: Flat diffuse with painted-on detail. No PBR.**

PBR creates physical conviction — "this is definitely real." Flat painted diffuse creates plausibility with uncertainty underneath. The horror operates in that uncertainty.

**Budget:** 512x512 for most props, 1024x1024 for large walls/floors. One 2048x2048 atlas per room category keeps draw calls within the 500 budget.

**Surface rules:**
- Painted walls: flat matte with faint brush-direction texture baked in
- Linoleum floors: color-blocked tiles with worn traffic paths in the diffuse
- Furniture: two-tone flat color (surface lighter, edges darker). Grain in three painted strokes, never photographed
- Crayon drawings/construction paper: high saturation, pure flat color, zero AO. Cut-out-and-taped look
- Metal: slight warm sheen as a highlight band painted into diffuse. No metallic shader

### 6.3 Prop Density Rules

| Room Type | Night 1 Density | Driver |
|---|---|---|
| Classroom | High | Children's materials in use |
| Art Room | Very High | Active creative chaos, mid-project state |
| Cafeteria | Low-medium | Institutional, adult-controlled, cleaned daily |
| Nap Room | Very Low | Sparse by design |
| Hallway | Low | Traffic path, kept clear |
| Playground | Medium | Weather-scattered, play structures anchor |
| Basement | Low | Adult-access only, utilitarian |

**Night-scaling:** Each night, anomaly props are added without removing baseline props. By Night 5-7, rooms feel subtly more crowded — same floor plan, too many chairs, drawings pinned over other drawings, toys that were put away back out.

**Solo dev rule:** Fixed prop set (always present) + anomaly pool (same assets in wrong states via scene instance swaps). No new art required for anomaly states.

### 6.4 Environmental Storytelling Guidelines

**Rule: Tell only what the camera could confirm. No notes, journals, or readable documents in the preschool.** Objects imply; the camera confirms; the boss interprets.

- **Abrupt absence:** Cubby with name tag torn half-off. Height-sticker that stops being updated.
- **Adult intrusion:** Coffee mug on child-height table. Cigarette ash under a window. Adult has been here after hours.
- **Cyclical marks:** Same crayon scribble in the same corner of three rooms. Drawn by someone small enough to reach only that height.
- **Maintenance that stopped:** Un-erased lesson on whiteboard. Lunch menu three weeks old. Hallway clock stopped at 4:17.
- **Child-scale evidence of wrong things:** Fingerprints on a ceiling tile no child could reach. A drawing of something children should not know. Crayon marks that follow a path rather than a shape.

### 6.5 Room Identity Rules

Each room has one identity color, shape, sound, and prop that persists across all seven nights.

| Room | Identity Color | Identity Shape | Identity Prop | Night 1 Feel |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Classroom | Crayola Yellow | Rows of small chairs | Wall-mounted alphabet strip | "Familiar. Almost like remembering school." |
| Art Room | Crayola Red (sustained) | Drying racks | Finger-painted mural (full wall) | "Cheerful and messy. The most human room." |
| Cafeteria | Chalk White | Long rectangular tables | Serving counter with sneeze guard | "Institutional. Slightly cold even on Night 1." |
| Nap Room | Crayola Blue | Low cots in rows | A single stuffed rabbit on one cot | "Quiet. Vulnerable." |
| Hallway | Crayola Green | Cubby wall (full length) | Height-measurement strip on door frame | "In-between. Nowhere to hide." |
| Playground | Mixed (outdoor) | Climbing structure | Sandbox with abandoned shovel | "Open. Wrong to be in at night." |
| Basement | Deep Shadow / Infection Violet | Exposed pipes and ductwork | Industrial shelf unit | "Not built for children." |

Identity props serve as calibration objects — anomalies involving the identity prop land hardest. The rabbit in the wrong cot. The shovel standing upright. The height-measurement strip showing measurements above the door. Anchor prop anomalies should land Night 3+ after the player has learned "normal."

### 6.6 Modular Construction Notes

**Core philosophy: 12-15 modular pieces assemble all 7 rooms.**

| Module | Variants | Used In |
|---|---|---|
| Wall panel, flat (2m) | 5 paint colors | All rooms |
| Wall panel with window | 2 sizes | Classroom, hallway |
| Wall panel with bulletin board | 1 | Classroom, art room, hallway |
| Corner connector | 1 | All rooms |
| Floor tile (1m x 1m) | 3 colors | All indoor rooms |
| Ceiling panel, flat | 1 | All indoor rooms |
| Fluorescent fixture | 1 | All indoor rooms |
| Door (child-scaled arch) | 1 | All rooms |
| Cubby unit (4-wide) | 1 | Hallway, classroom |
| Baseboard with trim | 1 | All rooms |
| Exterior wall panel | 1 | Playground perimeter |

**Assembly:** 1m grid snap. All wall panels are multiples of 1m wide. Rapid layout iteration in Godot editor without visual seams.

**Lighting as differentiator:** Same structural shell reads differently under warm practicals (classroom) vs. cold fluorescents (cafeteria) vs. zero overhead (basement). Build lighting rigs as scene resources, swap by room.

**Night state management:** Each room is a Godot scene. Anomaly states are child nodes toggled by the night system — not new scenes. Baseline prop set always present; anomaly nodes layer on top. Keeps asset count flat and web export sizes manageable.

## 7. UI/HUD Visual Direction

### 7.1 Diegetic vs. Screen-Space Split

| Register | Type | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Preschool HUD** (inventory, night counter, film counter) | Screen-space | The investigator's mental model — exists in the investigator's head, not the room |
| **Camera viewfinder** (frame lines, zoom, flash charge, anomaly lock) | Diegetic (camera body) | Physically exists on the camera. The UI is the device. |
| **Boss debrief** (evidence grades, dialogue, score, directives) | Screen-space, theatrical | A constructed presentation — the boss controls this space |

**What is never on screen:** Health. Stamina. Threat proximity meters. The player reads danger through visual and audio cues from the world. Removing these forces the player to trust — or distrust — what they see.

### 7.2 Typography Direction

Three typeface personalities, never interchangeable.

**Preschool HUD — "Crayon Print"**
Recommended: **Fredoka One** (Google Fonts, free) or **Baloo 2**. Rounded, monoweight, chunky.
Size floor: 18px at 1080p. Color follows Color Debt.

**Camera Viewfinder — "Etched Monospace"**
Recommended: **Share Tech Mono** (Google Fonts, free) or **JetBrains Mono Light**. Fixed-width, minimal.
Size floor: 14px at 1080p. Color: warm cream `#D4C8A0`, never white.

**Boss Debrief — "Authority Serif"**
Recommended: **Lora** (Google Fonts, free) or **Playfair Display**. Old-style serif with humanist warmth. On second playthrough, the officiousness reads as weaponized.
Headers: 24px+. Body: 18px. Grade stamps: 48px+. Color: `#D4A855` body, `#8B2020` headers.

**Rule:** No typeface from one register ever appears in another.

### 7.3 Iconography Style

**Preschool HUD:** Flat, filled, construction-paper cut-out quality. No outlines. Chunky single-color silhouettes.

**Viewfinder:** Line-only, single-pixel weight. No fill. Technical diagram aesthetic — etched or printed on equipment.

**Boss debrief:** Stamped graphic marks — grade letters in a round stamp border. "Rejected" stamp should feel like a physical impact, not a UI animation.

**Cross-register contamination:** Monster-register icons (`#F0F0FF`) reserved exclusively for anomaly lock in viewfinder. Any appearance elsewhere is a scripted horror beat.

### 7.4 Animation and Transition Feel

**Preschool HUD:** Short ease-out (120ms). Tacked up / peeled off. No slide-in.

**Camera viewfinder:** Zero animation on entry — present instantly when camera is raised. Only animated element: anomaly lock pulse (1.5s sine, 70-100% opacity).

**Boss debrief:** 0.5s hold on black, then single cut. Grade stamps: single-frame impact. Evidence cards: gentle upward drift (200ms ease-out, 8px travel).

**Menus:** Slow dissolve (250ms). No wipes, no slides. Horror lives in stillness.

### 7.5 Pillar 3 — "Trust No One" in UI Design

The boss debrief UI carries the full weight of Pillar 3. The preschool HUD and viewfinder are honest systems. The debrief is not.

**Surface read (first playthrough):** Warm professional evaluation — amber text, serif authority font, official grade stamps. "A fair system. A trustworthy employer."

**Embedded distrust signals (readable on replay):**
1. **Grade stamps use monster palette.** "Rejected" is Arterial Red `#C0181E` — the exact color of monster wounds. Authority and threats share a color vocabulary.
2. **Header color bleeds monster register.** `#8B2020` dark red accents — below conscious notice first playthrough, every boss header underlined in the color of what hunts the player.
3. **Grading scale escalates toward monster palette.** The better the evidence, the further from monster register. The "best" grade (green) is safe; the "worst" (Arterial Red) is dangerous. The rubric's color logic accuses the boss.
4. **Dialogue amber decays.** Boss text shifts from `#D4A855` toward `#8A6830` each night. Color Debt applied to his communication.
5. **Layout control.** Full-screen boss UI — no environment visible. A power move. Compare to the viewfinder, which the player controls and can lower at any time.

### 7.6 Accessibility

- **HUD text:** 18px minimum at 1080p, scales with resolution
- **Viewfinder:** 14px minimum — spatial, not story-critical
- **Boss debrief:** 18px body, 48px grades. Dark red headers (`#8B2020`, ~2.8:1 contrast) intentionally low — not used for critical information
- **Contrast ratios:** All primary text/icons minimum 4.5:1 (WCAG AA). Camera HUD cream on dark: ~7:1 (AAA).
- **Interaction feedback:** All interactable elements produce paired sound + visual cue. No interaction is silent-only or visual-only.
- **Colorblind:** Inherits full backup matrix from Section 4.6. All grades include letter labels. All warnings include shape and audio backups.

## 8. Asset Standards

### 8.1 File Formats

| Category | Source Format | Godot Import | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Meshes | `.blend` | `.glb` (GLTF 2.0) | Single file: mesh + skeleton + animations |
| Textures | `.png` | Basis Universal (`.ctex`) | Engine compresses on import |
| Materials | — | `.tres` (Godot Resource) | Shared resources, not per-mesh duplicates |
| Scenes/Rooms | — | `.tscn` (Godot Scene) | One per room; modular pieces as sub-scenes |
| Scripts | — | `.gd` (GDScript) | — |
| Audio SFX | `.wav` (source) | OGG Vorbis | Never ship .wav |
| Audio Music | `.wav`/`.flac` | OGG Vorbis | OGG for broadest WebGL 2 support |

### 8.2 Naming Conventions

All names: `snake_case`. No spaces, no uppercase.

**Assets:** `[category]_[description]_[variant].[ext]`
- `prop_chair_small.glb`, `prop_chair_small_d.png`
- `env_wall_flat.glb`, `char_doll_idle_a.glb`

**Texture suffixes:** `_d` (diffuse), `_e` (emission), `_a` (atlas)

**Materials:** `mat_[surface]_[variant].tres` — e.g., `mat_env_wall_yellow.tres`, `mat_monster_void.tres`

**Scenes:** `room_[name].tscn`, `mod_[module].tscn`, `char_[archetype].tscn`

**Scripts:** `[noun]_[role].gd` — e.g., `anomaly_manager.gd`, `photo_evaluator.gd`

**Node names:** PascalCase matching scene root — e.g., `ModWallFlat`

### 8.3 Texture Resolution Tiers

| Tier | Resolution | Per-Asset Budget | Used For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Micro | 128x128 | ~21 KB | Decals, small UI icons |
| Small | 256x256 | ~85 KB | Small props (blocks, cups, toys) |
| Standard | 512x512 | ~341 KB | Most props (chairs, tables, cubbies) |
| Large | 1024x1024 | ~1.4 MB | Walls, floors, ceiling panels |
| Atlas | 2048x2048 | ~5.5 MB | One per room category |

**Atlas categories (one 2048 atlas each):** classroom, art room, cafeteria, nap room, hallway, playground, basement, shared structural.

**Monster textures:** None. Flat-color `StandardMaterial3D` with albedo set to hex value only. Zero texture memory cost.

### 8.4 Polygon Count Budgets

**Room total (geometry + active monsters): ≤ 15,000 triangles**

| Category | Budget | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Structural (walls, floor, ceiling) | 1,500-2,000 tris | Flat quads, no subdivision |
| Large furniture | 80-150 tris each | Boxy, 1 chamfer loop |
| Small props | 20-60 tris each | Silhouette only |
| Decorative/flat (drawings, signs) | 2-6 tris each | Single quad |
| Modular pieces | 40-120 tris each | ~80 tri average |

| Shadow Figures | 200-300 / 150 / 80 | LOD 0/1/2 |
| Larger Creatures | 600-900 / 400 / 200 | LOD 0/1/2 |
| Boss | 300-500 | No LOD (fixed distance) |

**Meshes (.glb):**
- Generate LODs: Enabled | Shadow Meshes: Disabled (flat-shaded, no benefit)

**Download size:**

|---|---|

| Audio data | ≤ 20 MB |
| Code + scenes | ≤ 10 MB |

- Shadow quality: Low (1024x1024 max directional shadow)

## 9. Reference Direction

### Yume Nikki (2004, Kikiyama)

**Draw from:** Placing mundane, childlike objects exactly one degree off-center — wrong scale, wrong number of legs, wrong saturation — so corruption reads as a memory error rather than a monster. Objects recognizably themselves while being dimensionally wrong.

**Avoid:** Dream-logic non-sequitur. Everything in Show & Tell must feel physically grounded in a real preschool. Surrealism without spatial coherence breaks the "familiar shapes" rule.

**Why:** The purest demonstration that player-felt horror is produced by proportional wrongness, not explicit threat. That is this game's central mechanism.

### Fatal Frame II: Crimson Butterfly (2003, Tecmo)

**Draw from:** The compositional rule that danger is framed by the camera's viewfinder before the player consciously recognizes it — the ghost visible in the frame before it's visible to the naked eye. Camera as truth-filter.

**Avoid:** Supernatural lens distortion, ghostly double-exposure effects, fog-of-death visual language. Show & Tell's camera is a polaroid, not a spirit detector. It confirms, it doesn't hunt.

**Why:** Fatal Frame established the still-camera-as-weapon grammar. Show & Tell inherits and inverts it: the camera is evidence, not combat.

### Alice Neel, "Hartley" (1966) — Figurative Painting

**Draw from:** Neel's technique of distorting facial geometry just past the threshold of portraiture accuracy — proportions that read as the painter's error rather than the subject's deformity — while keeping color fully saturated and warm. Wrongness that feels like an unreliable witness.

**Avoid:** Expressionist distortion for emotional effect. Neel's wrongness is quiet and almost accidental-looking.

**Why:** The Living Dolls specifically need this reference. Their faces must read as attempted realism that fell short — not stylized horror.

### Phasmophobia (2020, Kinetic Games)

**Draw from:** Using environmental displacement as evidence of presence — furniture displaced by 3-5 degrees, doors left open that were closed. The room tells you something was here.

**Avoid:** Jump scare lighting — the sudden darkness/brightness flash used as ghost-proximity signal. Show & Tell's lighting decays slowly, never strobes for threat.

**Why:** Phasmophobia trained players to read environments as witnesses. Show & Tell inherits that literacy.

### 8 Passengers (Documentary Footage, 2023)

**Draw from:** The visual grammar of a space immaculately presented for an external audience while negative space — what is absent, what is not shown — signals the presentation is a performance. Forced normalcy as horror. Every shelf labeled, every activity in a designated bin. The order itself is wrong.

**Avoid:** Documentary framing, handheld shakiness, confessional-camera aesthetic. Show & Tell is not found footage. It is a space decorated for documentation, not a document of the space.

**Why:** Direct visual source for the preschool's surface presentation. The classroom should look staged for a home-school blog. That staging is the wrongness.
